# Lithography — Technologies, Equipment, Vendors, and Roadmap

Lithography is the patterning heart of semiconductor manufacturing — the step that defines the smallest features on a chip and therefore sets the ceiling on transistor density. It is the single most expensive equipment category, the most concentrated (one EUV supplier worldwide), and the most strategically contested. This file covers the physics, the major technology families (193i, EUV, High-NA EUV, NIL, DSA, e-beam), computational lithography, the vendor landscape, the patent and litigation history, and the roadmap to the 1nm class.

---

## 1. Fundamentals of Optical Lithography

Photolithography projects an image of a mask (reticle) onto a photoresist-coated wafer, then chemically develops the exposed pattern. The achievable resolution is governed by the **Rayleigh criterion**:

> **Resolution (half-pitch) = k₁ · λ / NA**

where:
- **λ** is the exposure wavelength,
- **NA** is the numerical aperture of the projection optics (NA = n·sinθ, with n the refractive index of the medium),
- **k₁** is a process-dependent factor capturing resolution-enhancement techniques (RET), with a hard single-exposure physical floor of **0.25** (and ~0.5 practically without aggressive RET).

Depth of focus (DoF) scales as **DoF = k₂ · λ / NA²**, which means that increasing NA to improve resolution shrinks the usable focus window — a central engineering tension.

Historically, resolution improved by **shrinking λ**: g-line (436nm) → i-line (365nm) → KrF (248nm) → ArF (193nm) → EUV (13.5nm). When wavelength scaling stalled at 193nm for over a decade, the industry instead **raised NA** (immersion) and **lowered k₁** (multi-patterning, OPC, off-axis illumination) to keep shrinking features.

---

## 2. ArF Immersion (193i) — The Workhorse

When the planned 157nm (F₂ laser) generation failed, the industry extended 193nm ArF lithography through **immersion**: placing ultrapure water (n≈1.44) between the final lens and the wafer raises the effective NA from ~0.93 (dry) to **1.35**. ArF immersion (193i), introduced around 2007 (45nm), became — and remains — the workhorse tool for the majority of layers even at the most advanced nodes.

To push below the ~38nm half-pitch single-exposure limit of 193i, the industry adopted **multi-patterning**:

- **LELE (Litho-Etch-Litho-Etch):** two separate exposures and etches interleave two patterns to double density. Extends to LELELE (triple) and beyond, but each pass adds cost and overlay error.
- **SADP (Self-Aligned Double Patterning):** a sacrificial "mandrel" is patterned, conformal spacers are deposited on its sidewalls, the mandrel is removed, and the spacers serve as an etch mask — yielding double the line density with excellent uniformity. **SAQP (Self-Aligned Quadruple Patterning)** repeats the spacer process to quadruple density; used for fins and tight metal layers at 10/7nm.
- **NTD (Negative-Tone Develop):** a resist/process scheme that improves contrast and process window for contact and trench layers.

Multi-patterning works but is expensive: a single critical layer can require 3–4 immersion passes plus the associated deposition and etch, multiplying cost and cycle time and accumulating overlay error. This cost is precisely what eventually justified EUV.

## 3. EUV Lithography (13.5nm) — The Decade-Defining Tool

Extreme ultraviolet lithography uses **13.5nm** light — a wavelength so short it is absorbed by virtually all materials, including air, glass, and conventional resists. This forces a radically different machine architecture, the product of ASML's >€10B, multi-decade development program.

### 3.1 The Light Source

EUV light is generated by **Laser-Produced Plasma (LPP)**: molten **tin droplets** (~25,000–50,000 per second) are hit by a high-power **CO₂ drive laser** (TRUMPF, ~30kW class). Each droplet is first hit by a pre-pulse to flatten it, then by a main pulse that ionizes it into a plasma at ~220,000 K, which radiates at 13.5nm. The light is collected by a grazing-incidence **collector mirror** and routed into the scanner. The source — originally Cymer's technology (ASML acquired Cymer in 2013) — was historically the gating factor for throughput; reaching ~250W of usable EUV power was the key milestone that made HVM viable.

### 3.2 The Optics

Because no material transmits 13.5nm, EUV uses **all-reflective optics**: precision mirrors coated with **40–50 alternating Mo/Si multilayer** pairs that create constructive interference to reflect ~70% at 13.5nm. ZEISS manufactures these mirrors to sub-nanometer figure accuracy — the most precise optical surfaces ever made at scale. The entire light path operates in **high vacuum**.

### 3.3 The Reticle (Mask)

EUV masks are also reflective: a Mo/Si multilayer mirror on a low-thermal-expansion substrate, with a patterned **TaN/TaBN absorber** defining the circuit. Key challenges:
- **3D mask effects:** the finite thickness of the absorber and the oblique illumination (6° chief ray angle) cause shadowing and image asymmetry. Research into **high-n / low-thickness absorbers** (e.g., Ni-, Co-, Ru-based) mitigates this.
- **The pellicle problem:** a pellicle is a thin membrane protecting the mask from particles. At EUV power levels, the pellicle must transmit ~90% at 13.5nm twice (in and out) while surviving immense heat. Polysilicon pellicles (~10–12nm) reach 88–90% transmission; **carbon-nanotube (CNT) pellicles** targeting >95% transmission and higher thermal tolerance are in development.

### 3.4 Resists and Stochastics

- **CAR (Chemically Amplified Resist):** the incumbent; a photoacid generator catalyzes deprotection. Limited by acid-diffusion blur and EUV's low photon count.
- **Metal-oxide resists (MOR):** tin-oxide-based resists (Inpria, acquired by JSR) absorb EUV directly in the metal, offering higher resolution and lower line-edge roughness, at the cost of sensitivity.
- **Stochastic effects:** because EUV photons are energetic and a useful dose delivers relatively few photons per pixel, **shot noise** causes random CD variation, **line-edge/width roughness (LER/LWR)**, and random "stochastic defects" (bridges and breaks). Managing the dose–LER–throughput trade-off is the central EUV process challenge (covered in depth in File 23).

### 3.5 Scanner Evolution and HVM Reality

ASML's NXE platform progressed from early low-power tools to the **NXE:3400C** (~170 wph) and **NXE:3600D** (~160–220 wph with improvements). Throughput, source power, and **availability (uptime)** — historically the Achilles' heel of EUV — improved steadily through the 2018–2023 ramp. TSMC's N7+ (2019) was the first HVM node to use EUV; N5 (2020) made it mainstream with 10+ EUV layers; N3 uses ~20+.

## 4. High-NA EUV (0.55 NA) — The Next Scanner

To extend EUV resolution without resorting to EUV multi-patterning, ASML developed **High-NA EUV**, raising NA from 0.33 to **0.55** on the new **EXE platform (EXE:5000, EXE:5200)**.

- **Anamorphic optics:** to keep mask shadowing manageable at the higher NA, the optics use **8× demagnification in the scan direction and 4× across the slit**. The consequence is that the printable field shrinks to **26mm × 16.5mm** (half the conventional 26×33mm field). Large chips must be **stitched** from multiple exposures — a significant design constraint for big GPUs.
- **Resolution:** enables ~8nm half-pitch single exposure, sufficient to push several nodes without EUV double-patterning.
- **Status:** the first EXE:5000 was installed at **IMEC (2023)** and at **Intel (2024)**, which targets first HVM High-NA insertion on **Intel 18A/14A**. Tool cost is ~**$380M+**, limiting early adoption to a few layers at the most advanced fabs. TSMC has signaled a more conservative, later adoption (A14 era).
- **Challenges:** thinner resists (<20nm) to control mask 3D and absorption, stitching accuracy <1nm, new metal-oxide resists, and the economics of a $380M tool delivering fewer dies per shot.

## 5. Alternative and Complementary Patterning Technologies

### 5.1 Nanoimprint Lithography (NIL)

NIL physically stamps a pattern into resist using a mask ("template") in direct contact, then cures it — eliminating optics entirely. **Canon's FPA-1200NZ2C** is the leading commercial system. Advantages: very low cost-per-layer, no expensive optics, sub-10nm resolution demonstrated. Disadvantages: defectivity (contact process), template wear, throughput, and overlay. Its first real production foothold is **3D NAND** (Kioxia/SK Hynix interest) where the regular, periodic patterns suit NIL; logic adoption remains a research question due to defect and overlay demands.

### 5.2 Directed Self-Assembly (DSA)

DSA uses **block copolymers** that microphase-separate into regular nanoscale domains, guided by a lithographic pre-pattern (graphoepitaxy = topographic guides; chemoepitaxy = chemical guides). It can multiply density (e.g., contact-hole shrink, line multiplication) and **rectify** the roughness of an EUV pre-pattern. Samsung has used DSA for DRAM contact/via shrink. Defect density (~10–100/cm²) remains the barrier to leading-edge logic. (Covered in depth in File 23.)

### 5.3 Electron-Beam Lithography

Direct-write e-beam offers maskless, arbitrary patterning at very high resolution, but single-beam throughput is far too low for HVM. Its roles:
- **Mask writing:** all photomasks (DUV and EUV) are written by e-beam tools (multi-beam mask writers from **IMS Nanofabrication**, majority-owned by Intel, and NuFlare/Toshiba).
- **Multi-beam direct write:** research and niche (ASML's former MAPPER program, now discontinued; multi-beam approaches for specialty/maskless applications).

## 6. Computational Lithography

As k₁ approached its limits, the **computation** behind each exposure became as important as the optics. Computational lithography pre-distorts the mask and tunes the illumination so the printed image matches the design despite diffraction:

- **OPC (Optical Proximity Correction):** modifies mask shapes (adding serifs, biasing edges) to compensate for diffraction and process effects.
- **SMO (Source-Mask Optimization):** jointly optimizes the illumination pupil and the mask pattern.
- **ILT (Inverse Lithography Technology):** computes the ideal (often curvilinear) mask shape by solving the inverse imaging problem — extremely compute-intensive but increasingly used, especially with curvilinear EUV masks.
- **AI / GPU acceleration:** **NVIDIA cuLitho** (announced 2023, with TSMC, ASML, and Synopsys) accelerates computational-lithography workloads — including ILT and RCWA-based simulation — by orders of magnitude on GPUs, addressing the exploding compute cost of mask synthesis at advanced nodes.

EDA leaders **Synopsys, Siemens EDA (Mentor Calibre), and ASML's Brion** dominate computational-litho software.

## 7. Vendor Landscape

| Vendor | Position |
|---|---|
| **ASML (Netherlands)** | The only EUV and High-NA supplier worldwide; also the leading DUV immersion supplier; owns Cymer (source), Brion (computational litho), HMI (e-beam inspection). The single most strategically important company in SemiCap. |
| **Nikon (Japan)** | Historically a leading DUV/immersion scanner maker; now a distant follower in immersion, focused on select customers and specialty; no EUV. |
| **Canon (Japan)** | DUV i-line/KrF scanners for mature nodes; the leader in **nanoimprint (NIL)**; no EUV. |
| **Cymer (now ASML)** | Pioneer of the LPP EUV light source; acquired by ASML in 2013. |
| **Gigaphoton (Japan)** | Independent DUV (and historically EUV-source) light-source supplier, primarily for Nikon/Canon scanners. |
| **ZEISS SMT (Germany)** | Exclusive supplier of EUV and High-NA projection optics to ASML; a co-equal technical partner (ASML took a stake in ZEISS SMT). |
| **TRUMPF (Germany)** | Supplier of the high-power CO₂ drive laser for the EUV source. |

ASML's monopoly arises from this tightly held ecosystem: even if a competitor obtained the patents, replicating the ZEISS optics, TRUMPF laser, Cymer source, and the integration know-how is a multi-decade undertaking.

## 8. Patent Landscape and Litigation History

- **EUV core IP:** ASML, ZEISS, and Cymer hold the foundational EUV patents (source, optics, reticle handling, dual-stage). Much early EUV IP traces to the U.S. **EUV LLC** consortium (late 1990s, involving Intel, Motorola, AMD, and national labs), whose licensing helped ASML lead.
- **ASML vs. Nikon (1990s–2004):** a long-running cross-patent dispute over immersion and scanner technology, settled in 2004 with cross-licensing and payments — clearing the way for the immersion era.
- **ASML vs. XTAL / Dongfang (2018–2019):** ASML's subsidiary Brion won a major trade-secret verdict (~$845M, later affecting XTAL's bankruptcy) against XTAL, a startup accused of misappropriating computational-litho source code — a case with China-linked dimensions that foreshadowed later IP-security concerns.
- **Patent expiry:** several foundational EUV-era patents begin expiring in the **2025–2030** window, but the practical barrier to entry remains the integrated know-how and supply ecosystem, not the patents alone.

## 9. Roadmap: Toward the 1nm Class

- **EUV layer expansion:** N3 uses ~20+ EUV layers; N2 and Intel 18A push further, with **EUV double-patterning (LELE-EUV / SAOP)** appearing for the tightest pitches until High-NA single-exposure takes over.
- **High-NA adoption curve:** Intel 18A/14A first (limited layers, 2025+); TSMC A16/A14 later (2026–2028); broad High-NA usage for critical layers by ~2028–2030.
- **Backside power delivery (BSPDN)** reshapes lithography by **freeing frontside metal layers** (power moves to the backside), but adds new patterning challenges: backside via lithography aligned to frontside transistors through a thinned wafer (IR alignment), and ultra-tight overlay requirements.
- **Mask infrastructure:** EUV mask-blank supply (AGC, Shin-Etsu, Hoya — only three suppliers) and **actinic mask inspection** (ZEISS AIMS EUV) are critical bottlenecks; stochastic-defect detection at 2nm pitch demands faster actinic inspection.
- **Overlay** requirements tighten to <2nm, driving the metrology side (ASML YieldStar, KLA Archer) as hard as the scanners themselves.
- **The k₁ wall and 3D:** as planar lithographic scaling approaches its economic and physical limits, density increasingly comes from **3D integration (CFET, stacking, advanced packaging)** rather than further 2D shrink — meaning lithography's role evolves from "shrink everything" toward "pattern the critical layers, and let architecture and packaging do the rest."

Lithography thus remains the pacing technology of the industry: whoever controls the most advanced scanner controls the leading edge, which is precisely why a single Dutch company sits at the center of global technology geopolitics.

## Extended Analysis: The Economics of Patterning and the High-NA Transition

### A. The Cost-Crossover Logic That Governs Lithography Choices

Every patterning decision in a modern fab is governed by a **cost-crossover calculation** that determines, layer by layer, which technique is cheapest. For a given layer at a given pitch, the choices are: a single EUV exposure (~$150M tool, but one pass), EUV multi-patterning (two or more EUV passes), DUV multi-patterning (multiple ~$80M immersion passes plus the associated deposition and etch for each spacer or cut), or High-NA EUV (~$380M tool, one pass, but half the field). The cost of each approach depends on the number of passes, the tool cost and throughput, the added deposition/etch steps, the overlay error accumulated, and the yield impact. A layer requiring three or four DUV immersion passes (each with its own deposition, etch, and overlay error) becomes cheaper as a single EUV exposure once EUV throughput and uptime are adequate — which is exactly the crossover that drove EUV adoption. The same logic now governs the **High-NA transition**: High-NA becomes economical first on the layers requiring the most EUV multi-patterning passes, where a single (expensive) High-NA exposure beats two or more standard-EUV exposures. This cost-crossover logic, applied layer by layer, explains the gradual, selective insertion of each new lithography technology — fabs do not switch wholesale but adopt each new method first where its economics are most compelling, then expand as it matures.

### B. The Multi-Patterning Burden and Why EUV Mattered

To appreciate why EUV was worth its enormous cost and decade-plus development, consider the **multi-patterning burden** it relieved. At the 10nm/7nm nodes, before EUV, the tightest layers required **self-aligned quadruple patterning (SAQP)** or multiple **LELE** passes — meaning a single layer could require four or more immersion exposures, each accompanied by spacer deposition, etch, and cut steps, accumulating overlay error and cost at every pass. A layer that EUV can pattern in one exposure might require a dozen or more individual process steps via DUV multi-patterning. EUV collapsed this complexity: one exposure replacing many, with less accumulated overlay error and fewer steps, despite the scanner's cost and the stochastic and throughput challenges. The savings in process steps, cycle time, and overlay error — not just the resolution itself — are what justified EUV, and they are why EUV adoption accelerated once the technology became reliable enough. This is also why EUV's value rises with the number of layers it can pattern in a single exposure, and why the industry continually pushes to move more layers from multi-patterning to single-exposure EUV (and eventually High-NA).

### C. The High-NA Field-Size Trade-Off

High-NA EUV's most consequential characteristic — and its most significant drawback — is the **halved field size** resulting from its anamorphic optics (26mm × 16.5mm instead of 26mm × 33mm). This is a direct consequence of the physics: achieving 0.55 NA while keeping mask-shadowing effects manageable required the anamorphic (8×/4×) demagnification, which halves the printable field in one dimension. The implications are significant and specific. Large chips — particularly the big GPU and AI-accelerator compute dies that already push against the reticle limit — exceed the High-NA field and must be **stitched** from multiple exposures, with stitching error held below ~1nm at the field boundary. This is a real design constraint: a chip's active compute array may have to be split at a field boundary, requiring careful design and adding process complexity. The field-size trade-off, combined with the $380M+ tool cost, is why High-NA adoption is initially selective (a few critical layers at the most advanced fabs, Intel leading) rather than wholesale, and why the cost-crossover and design implications of High-NA are being carefully weighed by each manufacturer. It is a vivid example of how a lithography advance brings not just new capability but new constraints that ripple into chip design.

### D. The Mask and Resist Ecosystem as the Hidden Bottleneck

A theme that recurs across the lithography roadmap is that the **scanner is only part of the system** — the mask, resist, pellicle, and inspection ecosystem is equally critical and often the hidden bottleneck. EUV mask blanks come from just three suppliers (AGC, Shin-Etsu, Hoya) and must meet extraordinary defect specifications; pellicles must transmit ~90% at high power without degrading (driving the CNT-pellicle development); resists must resolve the pitch with acceptable stochastics (driving the metal-oxide-resist development, especially for High-NA's sub-16nm pitches and sub-20nm-thin resists); and actinic mask inspection (ZEISS AIMS EUV) must catch EUV-relevant and stochastic mask defects at throughputs that are currently limited. Any one of these — a mask-blank shortage, an inadequate pellicle, a resist that cannot resolve the pitch, an inspection that cannot find the defect — can gate the deployment of a new lithography generation as surely as the scanner itself. This is why lithography is best understood as a **co-engineered system** spanning scanner, mask, resist, pellicle, and inspection, and why advancing the leading edge requires advancing all of them in concert — a coordination challenge that the EUV ecosystem (ASML, ZEISS, the mask and resist suppliers, and the fabs) has had to solve and must continue to solve for each new generation.

## Further Analysis: DUV's Enduring Role and the Patterning Toolbox

### A. Why DUV Immersion Remains the Workhorse

Amid the focus on EUV and High-NA, it is easy to forget that **DUV (ArF immersion, 193i) remains the workhorse of the entire industry** — the tool that patterns the majority of layers even at the most advanced nodes, and the dominant tool for the vast mature-node market. The reasons are economic and physical. Most layers on a chip do not require the finest resolution, and for those layers a single (or multi-patterned) DUV exposure is far cheaper than EUV — so even leading-edge fabs use DUV for the majority of their layers, reserving EUV for the critical few. The entire **mature-node market** (28nm and older, File 15) — which serves automotive, industrial, IoT, analog, power, and the vast majority of chips by volume — runs almost entirely on DUV, often on refurbished immersion scanners. This makes DUV an enormous, enduring market, and it is the segment where **Chinese domestic lithography (SMEE)** is focused (mature-node DUV being achievable without the EUV capability China lacks). The enduring role of DUV is a reminder that the leading edge, though it captures attention, is only part of the industry — and that the workhorse tools serving the bulk of production are as commercially important as the exotic EUV scanners serving the cutting edge.

### B. The Patterning Toolbox

A useful way to understand modern lithography is as a **toolbox** of complementary patterning techniques, deployed layer by layer according to the resolution and cost each layer requires. At the coarsest layers, single-exposure DUV (i-line, KrF, or ArF) suffices. At tighter pitches, DUV multi-patterning (LELE, SADP, SAQP) extends DUV's reach at increasing cost. At the critical layers of advanced nodes, single-exposure EUV replaces the multi-patterning, and at the very tightest, EUV multi-patterning or (emerging) High-NA EUV is required. Alongside these optical techniques sit the specialty tools: nanoimprint (for 3D NAND's regular patterns), directed self-assembly (for contact/via shrink and pitch multiplication, guided by an optical pre-pattern), and e-beam (for mask writing and specialty direct-write). The art of advanced patterning is selecting the optimal technique for each layer — balancing resolution, cost, overlay, and yield — and the trend is toward an increasingly **pluralistic toolbox** in which a single fab deploys many patterning approaches, each optimized for different layers and applications. This pluralism, rather than a single dominant technique, is likely to define the lithography endgame (File 23), as no one approach suffices for all the diverse patterning needs of a modern chip.

### C. Lithography as the Pacing Technology

The deepest point about lithography is that it remains the **pacing technology of the entire industry** — the capability that, more than any other, sets the limit on what can be manufactured and gates the leading edge. Whoever controls the most advanced lithography controls the leading edge: this is why ASML's EUV monopoly is the single most strategically important position in all of semiconductors, why EUV access is the central lever of export controls (File 18), and why the High-NA transition is so consequential. Lithography's pacing role flows from its place in the manufacturing flow: it defines the smallest features, which set transistor density, which bounds performance and power — so the resolution of the available scanner sets the ceiling on everything downstream. As scaling slows and turns three-dimensional, lithography's role evolves (from "shrink everything" toward "pattern the critical layers, and let architecture, materials, and packaging do the rest"), but it does not diminish: the critical layers still require the most advanced patterning, the overlay requirements tighten, and the new architectures (backside power, CFET) bring new patterning challenges. Lithography thus remains at the heart of the industry's progress and its geopolitics — the pacing technology, the strategic chokepoint, and the capability around which the entire equipment industry and the global contest for technological supremacy revolve.
